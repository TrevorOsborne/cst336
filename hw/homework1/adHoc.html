Ad-hoc Networking


In mobile and vehicular networks, the nodes join and dissociate from the network frequently. Ad hoc networks have been found most suitable for these situations. Researchers are trying to determine if the same approach could be used for UASs. Some of the primary concerns with using traditional ad hoc networks in mobile or vehicle nodes are UAV’s ability to travel randomly or in an organized swarm not only in 2 but in 3 dimensions. Vehicular nodes are constrained to 2-dimensional road travel.

Topology changes are much more frequent in UASs. UAV’s can malfunction and fall off the network or lose power and must land for recharging. Links may form and vanish because of nodes changing position. Mobile Ad hoc Network (MANET) stations that are locked on to a particular UAV for communication access would need to be transferred to another UAV seamlessly, without disrupting user sessions (Gupta, Jain, and Vaszkun, 2015). Researchers are still finding out the most effective routing protocol to handle these situations as well as a seamless handover procedure.
